Situation Update

Day 98 of the conflict. Day 59 of the ceasefire — strained but technically holding. The previous briefing's Key Watch asked whether Trump would deliver on his claim that progress could come "as early as this weekend." That window is now opening, and the answer so far is negative. Rather than a deal breakthrough, Friday brought a widening signal gap: Mohsen Rezaei, military adviser to Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei, told CNN that talks are at a "deadlock" and issued a concrete $24 billion frozen-asset demand — $12 billion upfront upon signing, the remainder over 60 days. He warned the US would "enter a dark corridor" if fighting resumes, and explicitly threatened to expand operations beyond the Gulf to the Indian Ocean, Bab al-Mandab, Red Sea, and Mediterranean. Trump's response was characteristically multidirectional: he revealed for the first time a rejected plan to send troops into Iran to collect enriched uranium ("nuclear dust"), declared the material now "entombed" and unnecessary to retrieve, and said he would be "honored" to meet Khamenei if a deal materializes. Maximum dissonance: Trump says deal is close while Iran says deadlock.

The Lebanon dimension fractured in a way not seen previously in this conflict. Lebanese President Joseph Aoun and PM Nawaf Salam both publicly accused Iran of using Lebanon as a "bargaining chip" in US negotiations — the sharpest Beirut-Tehran break since the war began. Hezbollah's Naim Qassem deepened his rejection, calling the Israel-Lebanon ceasefire proposal "absurd, humiliating, and shameful" and demanding full Israeli withdrawal as a precondition. The IDF confirmed killing Hezbollah engineering commander Abed Harb in a strike last week. Iran's foundational precondition for returning to the table — a Lebanon ceasefire — now faces opposition from both Hezbollah (which rejects the terms) and Lebanon's own government (which rejects Iran's framing of the linkage). The previous briefing's Risk Alert on Lebanon escalation remains live, and the three-way fracture (Israel-Lebanon government-Hezbollah) deepens instability.

A non-Iran shock is compounding geopolitical risk: May nonfarm payrolls crushed expectations at 172,000 vs. 80,000 consensus, with March and April revised upward by a combined 93,000 jobs. The 10-year yield surged to 4.54%, rate-hike probability jumped above 57% (up from ~50% pre-report), and the S&P is selling off ~1.6% with the Nasdaq down ~2.6% on semiconductor weakness (Micron -6.3%, Marvell -8%, AMD -6.3%). This creates a dangerous secondary risk layer: markets already softening from rate fears would absorb any geopolitical deterioration with significantly reduced cushion. The S&P's potential 10th consecutive winning week — which would have been the longest since 1985 — is in serious jeopardy.
