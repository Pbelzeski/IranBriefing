Situation Update

The landscape has undergone a seismic shift in the 1 day since the previous briefing. Trump did not execute the threatened Round 3 strikes. Instead, he reversed course entirely — posting on Truth Social late Thursday that he had "cancelled the scheduled strikes and bombings against Iran" because "discussions with the Islamic Republic of Iran have been brought to the highest level of Iranian leadership and approved." He told reporters in the Oval Office that a signing ceremony could happen "in Europe" within days. The Dow surged 930 points on the news, closing at 50,849. Brent crude cratered from ~$95 to ~$87 overnight, its largest single-day move in months.

But the triumphant narrative immediately began fracturing. Iran's Foreign Ministry spokesperson Esmail Baghaei told IRNA that reports of a finalized deal were "merely speculation" and that Tehran had "not reached a final conclusion." Then this morning, Iran's state news agency published what it described as seven key terms of the so-called "Islamabad MOU" — terms that are starkly contradictory to the US version leaked through Axios and CNBC. Iran's version claims no new nuclear agreements, no relinquishing of Hormuz control, and a US commitment to end Israel's Lebanon operations. Trump fired back, calling the Iranian terms "fake news" that have "NOTHING to do with the terms that were agreed to, in writing" and labeling Iranian negotiators "very dishonorable people." On the ground, the disconnect is even starker: US forces shot down two to four Iranian one-way attack drones targeting commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz early this morning, even as both sides publicly discuss a weekend signing ceremony in Geneva.

The previous Key Watch — whether Trump would execute Round 3 strikes and whether they'd target economic infrastructure — resolved decisively in favor of the Thunderclap thesis: Trump struck hard for two nights, then pivoted to demanding a deal. The previous Risk Alert regarding Kharg Island seizure has been downgraded as Trump moved from military maximalism to deal-mode. However, the new risk is arguably more insidious: two governments publicly claiming to have made a deal while publishing contradictory versions of what was agreed. Markets have priced in optimism — Brent down 8%, VIX down to 18.81 — but gold's 2.7% rise to $4,195 is a significant contrarian signal suggesting smart money is skeptical the deal will hold.

Two consecutive nights of CENTCOM strikes hitting targets within 40 miles of Tehran, combined with degraded air defenses and radar systems, demonstrated that the Islamic Republic cannot sustain continued military confrontation. The decision to engage diplomatically — even while the IRGC continues launching drones at Hormuz shipping — reflects the classic factional split: the diplomatic corps pursuing survival through negotiation while the IRGC maintains operational independence.

Economic pressure relief has risen to its highest priority level. The IRGC's continued drone attacks at Hormuz today signal that Iran's military establishment is either not aligned with the diplomatic track or is deliberately maintaining leverage to extract better terms before Sunday.
