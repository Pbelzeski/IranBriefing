Situation Update

The single most consequential development since yesterday's briefing landed at approximately 9:00 AM ET: Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi declared the Strait of Hormuz "completely open" for all commercial vessels, effective immediately, for the remaining period of the ceasefire. Markets exploded higher — Brent crude cratered over 10% from $94.89 to approximately $85, WTI hit $81, and the S&P 500 punched through 7,100 for the first time ever. The Dow surged roughly 1,000 points. Bond yields tumbled as traders repriced Fed rate-cut expectations. This is the single largest one-day oil move since the blockade began on April 13.

Critically, however, the Hormuz opening comes wrapped in contradictions that the market euphoria is ignoring. First, Iran's deputy FM Khatibzadeh — speaking at the Antalya Diplomacy Forum — stated flatly that Tehran is "not accepting any temporary ceasefire" and demands a comprehensive end to the conflict "from Lebanon to the Red Sea." Second, Trump immediately countered the Hormuz announcement by declaring the US naval blockade of Iranian ports "will remain in full force until our transaction with Iran is 100% complete." Third, Iran explicitly excluded naval vessels from the Hormuz opening, meaning the US Navy cannot transit without confrontation. The net effect: non-Iranian commercial shipping can flow through the Strait, relieving the global oil premium, but Iran itself remains blockaded and cannot export a single barrel.

Addressing the previous Key Watch: (1) The Lebanon ceasefire technically survived its first 15 hours but is already fraying — the Lebanese army reported "several Israeli attacks" including shelling of villages and fire directed at an ambulance team in southern Nabatieh. Israel's military spokesperson warned civilians to stay north of the Litani despite the truce. This is concerning but not yet a truce-breaking event. (2) Iran's response to Hegseth's "golden bridge or bombs" ultimatum came not as denunciation but as the dramatic Hormuz opening — a far more sophisticated move than a mere diplomatic statement. This validates the de-escalation pathway but does NOT indicate acceptance of US terms. (3) Round 2 Islamabad talks remain unconfirmed: Pakistan's Foreign Office says no date is fixed. Trump said talks could happen "this weekend" but provided no specifics. The absence of confirmation 4 days before ceasefire expiry is itself a negative signal.
